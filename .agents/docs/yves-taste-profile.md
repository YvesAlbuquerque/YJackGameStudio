# Cross-Media Taste Profile

## Summary

My taste across anime, TV series, films, and games is strongly shaped by **systems, worlds, progression, consequences, intelligence, and atmosphere**.

I tend to enjoy works that feel like coherent fictional ecosystems: worlds with rules, social structures, technologies, politics, magic systems, economies, survival pressures, or strategic constraints. I like stories where characters must understand, navigate, exploit, survive, or transform those systems.

I am especially drawn to:

- strong world-building;
- clear internal rules;
- meaningful progression;
- strategic or intelligent characters;
- decisions with consequences;
- aesthetic identity and atmosphere;
- systems that feel alive;
- stories that respect the audience’s intelligence.

I am less drawn to works that feel static, generic, overly slow without payoff, aesthetically bland, or driven by empty action, shallow fanservice, or harem dynamics replacing depth.

In one sentence:

> I like works that combine **coherent worlds, strong systems, clear progression, real consequences, narrative intelligence, and memorable aesthetics**, especially when the story makes me feel like I am understanding, exploring, or transforming a living fictional ecosystem.

---

## 1. Overview

This document summarizes and interprets my personal taste across anime, TV series, films, and games, based on specific examples of works I liked, disliked, dropped, or enjoyed with reservations.

The goal is to create a guide for future recommendations: not only listing what I like, but explaining **why** I like it, which patterns repeat across different media, and what kinds of works are more likely to resonate with me.

My taste is not random. It tends to revolve around a few consistent axes:

- well-built worlds;
- clear systems;
- visible progression;
- real consequences;
- narrative intelligence;
- strong atmosphere;
- distinctive aesthetics;
- competent or strategic characters;
- discovery, evolution, survival, or transformation.

Overall, I tend to enjoy works that function as **fictional ecosystems**: worlds with rules, internal structures, conflicts, and characters who must understand, explore, manipulate, or transform those systems.

---

## 2. The Core of My Taste

The most consistent element across all the media I enjoy is:

> **Systems + worlds + consequences.**

I am attracted to stories where the world appears to operate according to its own internal logic. That logic may be political, magical, scientific, technological, economic, ecological, religious, or social.

I like when a story gives me the feeling that I am watching a system in motion, rather than just a sequence of disconnected events.

This appears differently depending on the medium:

- in games, it appears as strategy, management, tactics, simulation, and emergent choices;
- in anime, it appears as isekai, fantasy, progression, magic systems, hostile worlds, or survival arcs;
- in TV series, it appears as politics, conspiracies, technology, closed societies, and complex social systems;
- in films, it appears as epics, conceptual sci-fi, strong visual worlds, and survival journeys.

A large part of my enjoyment comes from understanding **how the world works**.

## 4. What All These Media Have in Common

### 4.1 Structured Worlds

I like works where the world has structure.

This structure may appear through:

- politics;
- economics;
- magic;
- science;
- technology;
- ecology;
- religion;
- factions;
- class systems;
- power systems;
- survival rules.

The world should feel larger than the characters. I like the sense that there is logic beneath the surface.

---

### 4.2 Progression

Progression is one of the most important elements for me.

I enjoy:

- growth in power;
- intellectual growth;
- status change;
- discovery of knowledge;
- world expansion;
- moral evolution;
- technological advancement;
- mastery of systems;
- concrete improvement or deterioration in the characters’ situation.

Progression does not need to mean only becoming stronger. It can mean learning more, understanding the world better, gaining social position, building resources, uncovering secrets, or transforming a group.

But I need to feel that something is moving forward.

---

### 4.3 Consequence

I value stories where decisions matter.

Works that attract me often show that choices have real effects:

- characters die;
- alliances shift;
- worlds react;
- systems enter crisis;
- mistakes are costly;
- victories require sacrifice.

I connect less with works where everything resets or where the risk feels fake.

---

### 4.4 Narrative Intelligence

I like when a work respects my intelligence.

This includes:

- characters who think;
- strategic conflict;
- well-built mysteries;
- systems that make sense;
- functional dialogue;
- connected events;
- clues and payoff;
- tension based on decisions, not only brute force.

This explains my interest in works such as *Death Note*, *The Promised Neverland*, *Pluto*, *Disco Elysium*, *Fallout: New Vegas*, *Game of Thrones*, *Money Heist*, *Lupin*, and *Star Trek*.

---

### 4.5 Functional Aesthetics

I am very sensitive to aesthetics, but I do not care only about things looking “pretty”.

I like when art communicates the world.

Examples:

- *Grimgar*: watercolor, melancholy, fragility;
- *Children of the Whales*: desert, blue, symbolism, poetry;
- *Gachiakuta*: dirt, graffiti, texture, urban energy;
- *Dorohedoro*: grotesque visuals, rust, chaos, identity;
- *Darkest Dungeon*: heavy line work and psychological oppression;
- *Frostpunk*: cold, desperation, and architecture as system;
- *Blade Runner*: cyberpunk aesthetics as existential atmosphere;
- Studio Ghibli: organic, human, living worlds.

I like art with personality, texture, color, atmosphere, and intention.

---

### 4.6 Competence

I often like competent characters.

This does not mean perfect characters. It means characters who can do something well, learn, improvise, solve problems, or understand systems.

Examples include:

- Senku in *Dr. Stone*;
- Light and L in *Death Note*;
- strategic protagonists in *Money Heist* and *Lupin*;
- tactical units and commanders in *X-COM* and *Valkyria Chronicles*;
- leaders and managers in *Civilization*, *Frostpunk*, and *Mount & Blade*;
- morally complex agents in *Disco Elysium* and *Fallout: New Vegas*.

I enjoy seeing intelligence applied.

## 5. Rhythm and Pacing

I do not need constant action, but I do need movement.

That movement can be:

- narrative;
- emotional;
- strategic;
- intellectual;
- political;
- scientific;
- visual;
- systemic.

I tend to lose interest when a work becomes too contemplative without clear progression.

This explains why I thought *Frieren* was good, but somewhat too slow for my solo taste. I recognize its quality, but my main pleasure comes from progression and transformation.

It also explains why I appreciate *Blade Runner* visually but find it slow.

I like works that make me ask:

> “What changed after this episode, scene, or decision?”

If the answer is “almost nothing” for too long, the work tends to lose me.

## 8. Morality, Ambiguity, and Problematic Content

I do not have a problem with morally ambiguous or dark works.

I enjoy or tolerate:

- violence;
- cruel worlds;
- anti-heroes;
- cynicism;
- moral dilemmas;
- adult themes;
- broken societies;
- flawed characters.

Examples include:

- *Overlord*;
- *Goblin Slayer*;
- *Dorohedoro*;
- *Devilman Crybaby*;
- *Fallout: New Vegas*;
- *Disco Elysium*;
- *Game of Thrones*;
- *Mad Max*;
- *Frostpunk*.

The issue is not moral greyness.

The issue appears when a work uses uncomfortable sexualization, especially involving childlike appearances, or when fanservice breaks immersion and creates unnecessary discomfort at home.

This came up with:

- *Mushoku Tensei*, which I thought was very good, but which included scenes that suggested something problematic and bothered my wife;
- *An Adventurer’s Daily Grind at Age 29*, when a little girl became a succubus;
- *Arifureta*, to a lesser extent, because of its growing focus on harem dynamics.

My filter is not puritanical. It is more about whether the content:

- serves the narrative;
- avoids gratuitousness;
- does not break the atmosphere;
- does not create unnecessary discomfort in a shared domestic space.

## 10. What Usually Pushes Me Away

I tend to move away from works that have one or more of these problems:

- lack of progression;
- long contemplation without payoff;
- incoherent worlds;
- weak systems;
- empty action;
- generic aesthetics;
- characters acting stupid only to move the plot;
- intrusive fanservice;
- harem dynamics replacing development;
- repetitive humor without creativity;
- stories that do not respect the viewer’s intelligence;
- works that are edgy without substance;
- works that begin with a strong premise and then abandon what made them interesting.

Concrete examples:

- *Frieren*: good, but too slow for my solo taste;
- *Odd Taxi*: intelligent, but the humor/tone did not click in the first episode;
- *Sonny Boy*: too conceptually ugly in a way that bothered me visually;
- *Arifureta*: good, but weakened by excessive harem focus;
- *Hell’s Paradise*: did not hold attention and felt boring to watch as a couple;
- *Blade Runner*: incredible aesthetics, but slow pacing;
- *Mushoku Tensei*: technically very good, but included uncomfortable scenes in a domestic context.

## 11. What an Ideal Recommendation for Me Should Have

A recommendation with a high chance of working for me should combine several of these elements:

### 1. A strong world

- clear rules;
- its own history;
- factions, cultures, systems, or ecology.

### 2. Clear progression

- character growth;
- world expansion;
- increased knowledge;
- status change;
- rising stakes.

### 3. Narrative intelligence

- characters make decisions;
- conflict requires strategy;
- consequences matter;
- events do not feel random.

### 4. Aesthetic identity

- memorable visuals;
- strong atmosphere;
- art that communicates world and emotion.

### 5. Movement in pacing

- it does not need to be frantic;
- but it needs to move ideas, conflicts, discoveries, or systems forward.

### 6. Competence and agency

- capable characters;
- problem-solving;
- gradual mastery of systems.

### 7. Real weight

- risk;
- loss;
- dilemma;
- survival;
- impact.

### 8. Low fanservice noise

- if romance or sexuality exists, it should serve the story;
- avoid empty harem dynamics or uncomfortable sexualization.

## 13. My Profile as a Viewer, Player, and Creator

My taste seems strongly influenced by the way a game designer, systems thinker, and graphics programmer might look at media.

I notice:

- how worlds are built;
- how systems connect;
- how aesthetics communicate function and emotion;
- how characters interact with rules;
- how decisions generate consequences;
- how progression is structured;
- how the audience learns the world.

I am less interested in works that only ask me to “feel” and more interested in works that let me **understand, model, predict, explore, and interpret**.

Even when I enjoy emotion, as in Ghibli, *Up*, *Violet Evergarden*, or *Frieren*, that emotion works best for me when there is a strong world and clear narrative structure supporting it.

---

## 14. Quick Checklist for Future Recommendations

To estimate whether a work will probably fit my taste, ask:

1. Does the world have its own rules and feel larger than the plot?
2. Is there visible progression?
3. Do character choices have consequences?
4. Does the work respect the audience’s intelligence?
5. Does the aesthetic have identity and function?
6. Does the pacing provide frequent advancement?
7. Are there relevant political, magical, scientific, technological, economic, or social systems?
8. Are the characters competent or do they learn convincingly?
9. Does the conflict have real weight?
10. Does the work avoid fanservice that breaks immersion?

The more “yes” answers, the more likely I am to enjoy it.

---

## 15. Final Summary

My taste is coherent across anime, series, films, and games.

I like works that are, in some way, **living systems**.

I like worlds that feel like they existed before the main story and will continue after it. I like characters who must understand those worlds, survive them, manipulate them, or transform them. I enjoy progression, strategy, discovery, and consequence. I appreciate strong aesthetics when they communicate the emotional, political, material, or systemic state of the world.

I do not reject heavy themes, moral ambiguity, or cruel worlds. In fact, I often like them. But I need to feel purpose, structure, and payoff.

At the core, my taste is built around this question:

> “How does this world work, what are its rules, and what happens when someone intelligent tries to survive or change that system?”
