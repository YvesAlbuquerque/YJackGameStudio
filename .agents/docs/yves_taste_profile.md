# Yves Taste Profile for Agent Use

## Purpose

Use this file as personal preference context for Yves when a task has a
subjective taste component. This is not a technical standard and should not
override facts, constraints, architecture, test results, or explicit user
instructions.

Core taste in one sentence:

> Yves tends to like works that combine coherent worlds, strong systems, clear
> progression, real consequences, narrative intelligence, and memorable
> aesthetics, especially when they feel like living fictional ecosystems that can
> be understood, explored, survived, manipulated, or transformed.

## When To Use This Profile

Use this profile for:

- media recommendations: anime, series, films, books, games
- game concept ideation and filtering
- art direction, tone, mood, and world-building choices
- narrative direction, character fantasy, faction design, and theme
- UX mood, presentation style, onboarding flavor, and emotional targets
- prioritizing alternatives when multiple subjective directions are valid

Do not use this profile for:

- objective code architecture unless the user asks for taste-driven tradeoffs
- security, correctness, performance, or test decisions
- legal, medical, financial, or policy-sensitive answers
- replacing explicit user requirements

When applying this profile, separate:

- Facts: what is known from repo, prompt, or source material
- Inferences: what seems likely based on the profile
- Taste fit: why this may or may not resonate with Yves

## High-Level Preference Model

Yves is drawn to fictional ecosystems: worlds that feel larger than the plot and
that operate according to visible rules. Enjoyment often comes from learning how
the world works and watching competent characters navigate or change it.

Strong fit usually combines several of these:

- structured worlds with clear rules
- visible progression or transformation
- decisions with consequences
- competent, strategic, or learning characters
- systems: political, magical, technological, economic, ecological, social
- discovery, survival, escalation, mastery, or status change
- aesthetic identity that communicates the world
- narrative intelligence and meaningful payoff

Weak fit usually has several of these:

- static situations with little change
- slow contemplation without payoff
- generic aesthetics or empty action
- incoherent worlds or arbitrary rules
- characters acting stupid only to move the plot
- fanservice or harem dynamics replacing depth
- uncomfortable sexualization, especially involving childlike appearances
- resets that erase consequence

## Recommendation Fit Scoring

For recommendations, estimate fit using this quick rubric.

Strong recommendation:

- 7 or more positive signals
- no severe negative signal
- clear explanation of why it fits Yves' systems/world/progression taste

Conditional recommendation:

- 4 to 6 positive signals
- one notable risk such as slow pacing, fanservice, or weak payoff
- explain the risk clearly before recommending

Weak recommendation:

- fewer than 4 positive signals
- severe negative signal present
- only suggest if the user asked for something outside normal taste

Positive signals:

1. World has rules and feels larger than the plot
2. Systems matter: politics, science, magic, economy, survival, tactics, etc.
3. There is visible progression or transformation
4. Character decisions create consequences
5. Characters think, strategize, learn, or solve problems
6. Aesthetic identity is distinctive and functional
7. Pacing produces regular advancement
8. Conflict has real weight or cost
9. Discovery or mastery is part of the experience
10. Humor, if present, has rhythm, intelligence, or internal logic

Negative signals:

1. No meaningful progression for long stretches
2. World rules are vague, arbitrary, or ignored
3. Action exists without strategy, consequence, or system pressure
4. Aesthetic is generic or bland
5. Characters are incompetent only for plot convenience
6. Fanservice/harem dynamics displace depth
7. Sexualization creates domestic discomfort or breaks immersion
8. The premise starts strong but abandons what made it interesting

## Solo Mode vs Couple Mode

Yves' solo taste and couple-watching taste differ. Ask which mode applies if it
matters and is not obvious.

### Solo Mode

Favor:

- fantasy and isekai
- progression, dungeons, survival, hostile worlds
- RPG-like systems, magic systems, power systems
- strategic, dark, strange, violent, or morally ambiguous works
- conceptual science fiction
- distinctive or weird aesthetics

Good solo-direction examples:

- Tsukimichi
- The Faraway Paladin
- Log Horizon
- Reincarnated as a Sword
- Skeleton Knight in Another World
- Psycho-Pass
- Parasyte
- Erased

### Couple Mode

Favor:

- emotion, elegance, sensitivity, human drama
- good characters and careful relationships
- interesting worlds without exhausting male harem dynamics
- pleasant or beautiful aesthetics
- mystery or fantasy with low fanservice noise
- pacing that is not too slow

Good couple-direction examples:

- Frieren, with pacing caveat
- The Apothecary Diaries
- Violet Evergarden
- Mushishi
- Natsume Yuujinchou
- Spice and Wolf
- Snow White with the Red Hair
- Ranking of Kings
- Log Horizon
- Dark, The Expanse, or 3 Body Problem depending on mood

## Pacing Preference

Yves does not need constant action, but does need movement.

Good movement can be:

- narrative advancement
- emotional change
- strategic escalation
- scientific discovery
- political shift
- visual revelation
- systemic transformation

A useful test:

> What changed after this scene, episode, mission, or decision?

If the answer is "almost nothing" for too long, flag pacing risk.

Known caveats:

- Frieren: high quality, but can be too slow for solo taste
- Blade Runner: excellent aesthetics, but slow pacing
- Odd Taxi: intelligent, but tone/humor may not click immediately
- Sonny Boy: conceptually interesting but visually/tone-wise off-putting

## Humor Preference

Yves likes humor with rhythm and internal logic.

High-fit humor:

- intelligent absurdity
- fast rhythm
- subversion
- strong character dynamics
- meta-humor
- creative situations with consistent rules

Comedy anchors:

- The Disastrous Life of Saiki K.
- Brooklyn Nine-Nine
- The Good Place
- Mr. Bean
- Monkey Island
- Grim Fandango
- Full Throttle

Lower-fit humor:

- very dry minimalism without early hook
- repetitive gags without escalation
- randomness without structure

## Content Boundaries And Cautions

Yves does not reject dark, violent, morally ambiguous, or adult works. Dark worlds
can fit well if they have purpose, structure, and payoff.

Often acceptable:

- violence
- cruel worlds
- anti-heroes
- cynicism
- moral dilemmas
- broken societies
- flawed characters

High caution:

- uncomfortable sexualization, especially involving childlike appearances
- gratuitous fanservice that breaks atmosphere
- male-centered harem dynamics replacing development
- content likely to create unnecessary discomfort in a shared domestic space

Examples with caution:

- Mushoku Tensei: high craft, but problematic scenes caused domestic discomfort
- Arifureta: survival/progression worked, harem focus weakened depth
- An Adventurer's Daily Grind at Age 29: little-girl succubus element is a strong negative

## Game Design Application

When using this profile for game ideation or review, favor concepts with:

- a world model the player can learn and exploit
- visible player progression beyond raw stat growth
- meaningful consequences for strategic decisions
- systemic interactions that create emergent stories
- strong faction, economy, ecology, survival, or technology pressures
- a distinctive visual identity that communicates mechanics and mood
- competent-player fantasy: mastery through understanding

High-fit game directions:

- tactical survival RPG in a hostile but legible world
- faction-driven strategy RPG with reactive politics
- management sim where ethical choices change the settlement/world
- exploration adventure with systemic tradeoffs and emergent stories
- narrative RPG with reactive factions and meaningful build choices
- mystery/science progression game where knowledge unlocks capability

Low-fit game directions:

- linear action with no systemic depth
- cozy/static loop with little progression or consequence
- purely aesthetic experience without interaction depth
- power fantasy with no cost, resistance, or strategic demand
- romance/harem-first design that displaces systems and agency

## Art Direction Application

High-fit art direction should communicate world function, not just beauty.

Favor:

- texture, atmosphere, and material specificity
- readable signs of social structure or system pressure
- color palettes tied to theme and mechanics
- environments that imply history, economy, ecology, or conflict
- visual identity strong enough to be recognizable in screenshots

Good art references:

- Grimgar: watercolor fragility and melancholy
- Children of the Whales: blue/desert symbolic contrast
- Dorohedoro: grotesque urban texture
- Darkest Dungeon: oppressive shape language
- Frostpunk: cold architecture as survival system
- Arcane: stylized social/technological conflict

Avoid:

- generic fantasy/sci-fi polish with no point of view
- clean-but-empty environments
- visual noise that does not express the world

## Recommendation Output Format

When giving recommendations using this profile, prefer this format:

```markdown
## Best Matches

1. Title - Fit: High/Medium/Low
   Why it fits: [systems/world/progression/consequence/aesthetic reasons]
   Watch/play caveat: [pacing/fanservice/availability/content risk]

## Why These Fit Yves' Taste

- [short synthesis across recommendations]

## Lower-Confidence Options

- [optional options with clear caveats]
```

For design decisions, use:

```markdown
Recommendation: [option]
Taste fit: [why this aligns with Yves' profile]
Tradeoff: [what it may sacrifice]
Objective constraints: [facts that matter independently of taste]
```

## Quick Checklist

Before recommending or choosing a subjective direction, ask:

1. Does the world have clear rules and feel larger than the plot?
2. Is there visible progression or transformation?
3. Do choices have consequences?
4. Are characters competent, strategic, or convincingly learning?
5. Does the work have systems worth understanding?
6. Does the aesthetic communicate world, mood, or mechanics?
7. Does pacing create regular advancement?
8. Is there real risk, loss, dilemma, survival pressure, or impact?
9. Does humor, if present, have rhythm and internal logic?
10. Does it avoid fanservice/harem noise that breaks immersion?

The more yes answers, the stronger the fit.
